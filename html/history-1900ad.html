Pi: Paradise for number lovers

A history of pi-ness:

 

1900 to 2001

D.F Ferguson was the next to do anything major, in 1947 he discovered Pi to 808 digits, using a chunky calculator and an arctangent formula, this took him over a year to discover. But remember William Shanks spent a fair part of his life calculating Pi to 607 digits.

One year (1948) later Levi Smith and John Wrench confirmed Ferguson’s 808 digits and then decided to keep going and calculated Pi to 1000 digits. This took the two around 4 months.

Just one year later a computer called ENIAC was created by a US based company. Using this newfound speed, a team of researchers calculated Pi to 2,037 digits in only seventy hours, that's around 2 digits every minute. 5 years later the NORC (Naval Ordnance Research Calculator) was used to calculate Pi at about 4 digits a second, calculating a total of 3,089 digits in a mere 13 minutes.

Then in 1958 IBM came into play and calculated Pi to 707 digits in 40 seconds. They then went on to calculate pi to 10,000 digits in 1 hour 40 minutes. From here several mathematicians started competing with one another to calculate pi to the highest digit

starting with 100,256 in 1961 digits and ending with 1 million digits calculated in 1973. While all these leaps in discovering digits were major they were all using the same formula, so it was not who was the smartest and had the best formula but who had the biggest and fastest processor. It wasn’t until 1976 that someone created a new formula for calculating Pi. Eugene Salamin developed a new algorithm. Using Salamin’s algorithm Yoshiaki Tamura of Japan calculated pi to 8,388,608 digits, in 1982.

Over the next ten years two sets of brothers would feud over the king of Pi-ness. The Borwein brothers and the Chudnovsky Brothers.  These two sets of brothers followed the path of Salamin, creating high-powered algorithms to calculate pi. Which led to a race between the Chudnovsky brothers and Dr Kanada from 1988 to 1997. The two leap frogged each other in a race to calculate Pi the fastest and most precisely. Kanada was the first calculating 100 million digits, then the Chudnovskys found 525 million digits then Kanada found 536 million digits that same year which lead to the Chudnovsky finding Pi to it’s 1 billionth digit in that very same year (which was 1989). Then in 1995 the Kanada found Pi to it’s 6 billionth digit, then the Chudnovsky brothers found over 9 billion digits in 1996. The war of Pi-ness finally ended in 1997 when Kanda along with Takahashi calculated pi to 51.5 billion digits with a Hitachi SR2201 computer system.

Then in 1999 Kanda and Takahashi calculated pi to 206,158,430,000 digits using 2 different methods.
