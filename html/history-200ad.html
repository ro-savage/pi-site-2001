Pi: Paradise for number lovers

A history of pi-ness:

 

200 to 600 AD

China

In China mathematicians were also trying to discover the value of Pi, though they were slow. There was not a known attempt to calculate Pi till 600 years after Archimedes had said that Pi was 211875/67441. Ch’ang Hong, the Emperor’s an-ti minister and astrologer said around 200A.D that ‘The (circumference of a circle)² divided by the (perimeter of the circumscribed square)² = 5/8.’ If using this method we say that the diameter of the circle is 1cm, this means p²/16 = 5/8 when worked out we find Pi equals Ö10 (or about 3.162).

In Asia this became the most widely used formula for Pi. This is most likely because it is so simple. Yet this is quite inaccurate; take this example taken from “The Joy of Pi”.

 

If you tried to build a circular building 50 feet in diameter using the value Ö10, you’d mismeasure the circumference by 1 foot and the era of the floor plan by over 12 square feet.

 

Then in 263A.D (650 years after Antiphon and Bryson made there calculations) Lui Hui discovered the same method as Antiphon and Bryson; he used this method to create a 192-sided polygon. Using this method he published a book saying that Pi was between 3.141024 and 3.142704. Then sometime later using the same method he determined that pi was equal to 3.1416, using a 3,072-sided polygon.

Around 250 years (500A.D) later the astronomer Tsu Keng-chih inscribed a polygon with an amazing 24,576 sides! From this he deduced that pi was equivalent to 355/133 (about 3.1415929). This is amazingly only 8-millionths of 1 percent of the true value of Pi. No one would find a more accurate value for over one thousand years.

 

India

At the same time China was trying to discover the value of Pi, so was India.

Around 530 A.D a great Indian mathematician Aryabhata determined by an unknown method that Pi equals approximately Ö9.8684. He then wrote in a poem called Ganita

“Add 4 to 100, multiply by 8 and add 62,000. This is the approximate circumference of a circle whose diameter is 20,000.”

When you divide 62,832 by 20,000 the answer is 3.1416 which weirdly is more accurate than Ö9.8684.

A few other Indian mathematicians tried to calculate Pi, but none got closer than Pi = Ö10.
